Horses, donkeys and zebras have adaptations that break normal genomic rules

by Justin Jackson , Phys.org

A genetic mutation in horses that would typically halt protein production has become a molecular asset. Researchers at Johns Hopkins University and Vanderbilt University have identified a rare instance of genetic recoding that enhances oxygen metabolism and energy production in horses, donkeys, and zebras.

The findings, published in Science, provide insight into the genetic foundation of exceptional equine athletic ability, and hint at an entirely new way of dealing with stop codons.

Few mammals match horses in aerobic performance. Muscle tissue in thoroughbreds consumes oxygen at rates exceeding 360 liters per minute. Oxygen uptake per unit of body mass is more than twice that of elite human athletes. While many genes involved in muscle structure and locomotion have been studied, the genetic basis for this level of metabolic output has remained unclear.

Past research has focused largely on physical traits such as size, muscle architecture, or gait. Genomic studies have found mutations in genes affecting olfaction, keratin, and myosin, yet did not establish a direct link to aerobic metabolism.

In the current study, researchers focused on the gene KEAP1 and the KEAP1-NRF2 pathway, which regulates antioxidant responses and mitochondrial energy production.

In birds, this pathway evolved to manage the oxidative stress caused by flight. In early vertebrates, it helped them adapt to environmental stressors during the transition from water to land. Researchers have proposed that a similar mechanism may have contributed to athletic equine evolution.

In preparing for their study, the researchers came across a premature stop codon in equine versions of the gene compared to other species. Located unusually early in the protein sequence, the stop codon would be expected to end the transcription of the protein early, like stopping a 40-step assembly instruction after the first step. The resulting protein should be so truncated as to render it entirely nonfunctional.

Research that was meant to look into KEAP1 as a source of equine energy conversion suddenly had to confront the possibility that the gene, known to be critical to cellular redox balance and energy regulation, was not even coding. What they found instead was something truly unexpected.

In the study, "Running a genetic stop sign accelerates oxygen metabolism and energy production in horses," scientists used molecular biology and evolutionary analysis to investigate the premature stop codon in the KEAP1 gene. Genetic comparisons across all extant species of Equus were examined along with functional tests in horse and human cells.

The team combined genetic comparisons across living horse species with cellular and molecular experiments.

They used CRISPR-Cas9 to model genetic changes in human cells, mass spectrometry to examine protein structure, and metabolic assays to measure oxygen consumption and ATP production. Horse muscle and skin cells were studied alongside samples from other species to assess differences in energy output and oxidative stress responses.

What the researchers found challenged fundamental rules of genetics. The KEAP1 gene typically encodes a protein composed of more than 600 amino acids. A stop codon at position 15 would ordinarily halt translation near the beginning, producing only 14 amino acids. That length is far too short to support any of the gene's known roles in cellular defense or energy regulation. Yet in horse cells, the protein appeared fully translated, complete and functional.

Mass spectrometry revealed the explanation. The opal stop codon was being recoded as a cysteine, which allowed the translation process to continue. This rare form of genetic readthrough had not previously been documented in a gene like KEAP1 in any vertebrate. In horses, it enabled the cells to produce the full-length KEAP1 protein without interruption.

Analysis revealed a broad evolutionary change where KEAP1 and several components of the translation system had evolved uniquely in Equus, turning what should have been a fatal error into an evolutionary advantage.

Stop codons can sometimes be reinterpreted, allowing the cell to insert an amino acid rather than halting translation. This normally requires a specific signal in the mRNA that guides the ribosome to override the stop and insert selenocysteine. In horses, no such signal was found in the KEAP1 transcript. Yet the translation continued.

One likely explanation lies in the structure of the KEAP1 mRNA itself. The end of the transcript forms a distinctive shape that appears to attract the proteins needed for stop codon readthrough. This structure may act as an alternate signal, guiding the ribosome past the premature stop.

A second explanation comes from changes to the translation machinery itself. Two proteins involved in reading stop codons (SBP2 and eEFSec) carry mutations unique to Equus. Changes clustered in regions that help position transfer RNAs during translation.

Functional assays showed that the equine version of SBP2 alone was enough to drive recoding in a test system, even in the absence of the usual mRNA signals.

Together, the structural features in the transcript and the changes in translation factors appear to form a co-evolved solution that turns a genetic stop sign (selenocysteine) into a functional green light (cysteine).

The presence of the mutation in all modern equine species suggests it emerged early in the lineage—a change that may have allowed for increased aerobic performance, shaping the evolution of speed and endurance traits.

Roughly one in 10 human genetic disorders results from premature stop codons. A naturally occurring example of stop codon recoding that maintains balance while improving function may offer new directions for biomedical research with therapeutic approaches that aim to restore functional protein readthrough.
